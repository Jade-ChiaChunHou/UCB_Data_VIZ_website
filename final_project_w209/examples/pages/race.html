The focus of this project was to uncover relationships between racial and social factors and the impact of COVID-19. For this segment, we’ve looked at relationships between a county’s nonwhite population and its occurrence of COVID-19 cases and deaths. We found that this relationship has varied throughout the pandemic - early indications suggest that counties with high nonwhite populations were more adversely affected, but this trend has reversed as the pandemic has raged on, making the narrative less clear. These visualizations allow the user to observe this relationship over time to reach their own conclusions.



The next section focuses on the disparate impact COVID-19 has had on white and nonwhite populations in the United States. The data shows that throughout the course of the pandemic nonwhite Americans have been more likely to contract and die from COVID-19 than white Americans. This is true at the state level, where the vast majority of states report higher proportions of cases and deaths by nonwhites than would be predicted by their population sizes, and it is true at the county level where counties with higher nonwhite populations are likely to have greater numbers of cases and deaths than otherwise similar counties with lower nonwhite populations. This trend was particularly strong in the early days of the pandemic but has reversed in the last several months. In fact, in November counties with higher white populations were hit hardest by COVID-19, both with regards to contagion and lethality. All of these factors are part of the complex story of how COVID-19 has progressed and what populations have been impacted.

The analysis presented in this section should not be interpreted as a declaration of causality. There are many explanations for why nonwhite populations have borne the disproportional brunt of this virus, including geographic reasons, population density, and socioeconomic factors. This analysis is merely acknowledging the existence of this disparity and informing viewers of how COVID-19 has progressed in the United States.
